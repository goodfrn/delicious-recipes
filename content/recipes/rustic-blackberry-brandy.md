instructions:
- "==="
- "In a large sterile mason jar toss blackberries with raw sugar. Use a muddler or sturdy wooden spoon to crush berries thoroughly until sugar seems integrated and juice turns darker. The granular sugar texture should nearly vanish into syrupy pulp — tactile check essential here."
- "Pour bourbon over the mashed fruit and sugar. Drop in optional cinnamon stick for subtle spice note. Seal jar tightly. Shake vigorously just enough to blend layers without smashing fruit further."
- "Place jar away from sunlight in a cool spot. The infusion develops best in dim environments. Shake jar every 48–72 hours; aim for consistent mixing yet avoid over-agitation that breaks down solids too much and muddies liquid."
- "After approximately 13–15 days gauge by color deepening and slight sediment settling at jar bottom. Liquid should be richly purple and smell fragrant but not fermented or vinegary."
- "Strain mixture through fine mesh sieve lined with doubled cheesecloth holding back pulp. Press gently with spoon or spatula to extract as much flavor-rich liquid as possible without forcing through solids that cause grit."
- "Pour filtered brandy into a clean dry container. Discard pulp or compost it. Store sealed in fridge or cellar. Flavors mellow with storage but drinkable right away."
- "If cloudy or off smells develop—discard or restart. Use fresh fruit and keep tools clean to prevent spoilage. If a less sweet profile desired, reduce sugar or swap for honey but expect slower dissolution requiring longer muddling."

ingredientsNote: "Fresh seasonal blackberries always outshine frozen, but frozen can work if thawed completely without waterlogging. Brown sugar adds a deeper molasses edge beyond granulated. Honey is a viable alternative but dissolves slower, may require longer crushing and infusing. Bourbon chosen for its vanilla-caramel backbone instead of the neutral profile of brandy, but skip it if you want lighter tones. Cinnamon optional—try star anise or clove for variations but remember strong spices can overwhelm fruit's natural notes. Use a glass jar; avoid metal which can react with acids. Cleanliness is non-negotiable to prevent off-flavors or mold. If muddling gets tedious, pulse fruit in a blender very briefly, but watch texture to avoid over-pureeing."
