ingredients:
- "100g lightly softened unsalted butter"
- "2 tablespoons finely chopped fresh chives"
- "3 small garlic cloves grated or finely minced"
- "1 tablespoon whiskey (bourbon or rye)"
- "½ teaspoon coarse sea salt"
- "¼ teaspoon freshly ground black pepper"
- "Optional: 1 teaspoon lemon zest for brightness"
instructions:
- "Start with butter room temperature. Gives easier mixing without melting."
- "Combine butter, chives, garlic, whiskey, salt and pepper in a small bowl. Skip zest if you want pure savory."
- "Mash ingredients together with a fork or small spoon. Press, fold, twist motion. Look for fully incorporated blend with no streaks of garlic or butter lumps. Texture should be creamy but hold shape. Aroma will shift from raw garlic sharpness to gentle savory sweetness."
- "Shape butter into a small log or mound using parchment paper. This step speeds chill and stores better than a bowl."
- "Refrigerate 20 minutes minimum, ideally 30-40, until firm to touch but pliable. Check by pressing with fingertip. If too hard, leave out 3 minutes. Too soft? 10 more minutes fridge."
- "Serve room temp or slightly chilled on hot steak or grilled veggies. If spreading on bread, let sit 5 minutes after fridge to soften slightly."
- "Substitutions: Butter can be replaced with vegan buttery spread but check salt levels carefully. Whiskey can be swapped for dry sherry or aquavit for different smoky notes. If you skip alcohol, add ½ teaspoon apple cider vinegar or lemon juice to cut fat heaviness."
- "Mistakes: Avoid adding watery items like fresh herbs straight from rinsing – pat dry to prevent butter softening and spoilage. Don't mash too hard or then it'll melt. Use a fork rather than blender to keep air out and texture dense."

Fresh chives over dried. Dried will fall apart and lose freshness; chives cut finely or chopped hold more flavor. Garlic needs to be finely minced or grated—grating releases juices faster, helps blend flavor gently but don’t blitz it into a paste or it’ll become bitter. Whiskey here not going to cook out; better to use something mid-range, no harsh alcohol notes. You can swap with bourbon, rye, or even smoky mezcal for that complexity.
