ingredients:
- "3 tablespoons extra virgin olive oil, divided"
- "2 medium shallots, finely chopped"
- "3 garlic cloves, minced"
- "8 ounces shiitake mushrooms, sliced (substitute cremini for earthiness)"
- "¼ cup Marsala wine (alternative dry vermouth or white wine)"
- "1 cup fire-roasted cherry tomatoes, roughly chopped"
- "2 tablespoons green olives, pitted and sliced instead of capers"
- "1 package fresh lobster ravioli (about 12 pieces)"
- "4 tablespoons unsalted butter"
- "½ cup ricotta cheese, whipped"
- "Zest of 1 lemon"
- "1 tablespoon freshly squeezed lemon juice"
- "2 tablespoons toasted walnuts, chopped (replace pine nuts)"
- "Flaky sea salt to finish"
- "Freshly ground black pepper"
instructions:
- "Heat 2 tablespoons olive oil over medium heat in a large skillet. Add finely chopped shallots, sautéeing until just soft and translucent—look for gentle color change, about 3 minutes."
- "Add minced garlic, stir constantly to prevent burning. Once fragrant, roughly 60 seconds, add sliced shiitake mushrooms. Toss mushrooms to coat fully; they’ll release moisture and shrink. Cook until mostly dry and slightly golden, about 6–7 minutes. Listen for sizzle diminishing as moisture evaporates."
- "Deglaze skillet with Marsala wine, scraping bottom to free browned bits. Let alcohol cook off until pan looks almost dry, a minute or two."
- "Mix in roasted cherry tomatoes and sliced green olives. Their acidity and brine punch through richness. Remove pan from heat. Taste balance: acidity, salt, and sweet mushroom flavor should pop."
- "Boil a large pot of heavily salted water. Add lobster ravioli, cooking per package instructions. Fresh ravioli usually needs 3–4 minutes. When done, they float and have slight firmness with tender pasta. Drain carefully, drizzle with remaining olive oil to avoid sticking. Do not rinse — rinsing washes away flavor and makes sauce slide off."
- "Return clean skillet to medium-low. Melt butter slowly until foaming and just turning golden; careful not to burn. Butter’s nutty aroma signals readiness."
- "Using a slotted spoon or kitchen spider, transfer ravioli into butter sauce. Gently fold ravioli to coat evenly without tearing. Add any extra cooked seafood or protein now if desired—shrimp or scallops work well, warmed through."
- "Divide ravioli among four shallow bowls. Dollop with whipped ricotta, zest lemons directly over plates, and spritz with fresh lemon juice to brighten. Finish with toasted walnuts for crunch, flaky sea salt for texture contrast, and cracked black pepper for warmth."
- "If anything seems flat, add lemon juice or olive oil to adjust brightness and mouthfeel."

Mushrooms must cook until most liquid has evaporated—if too wet, sauces won’t cling properly and ravioli will slide around. Deglazing with Marsala is more than flavor; it loosens caramelized bits holding deep flavor. Fresh ravioli takes less time than dried — keep an eye; they’re done once floating gently.
