ingredients:
- "1 1/4 cups vegetable broth"
- "1 cup couscous"
- "3 tbsp fresh lime juice"
- "1 tsp lime zest"
- "1/2 tsp kosher salt"
- "3 tbsp olive oil"
- "1/4 cup fresh dill leaves chopped"
- "1/4 cup toasted pine nuts"
instructions:
- "1 Bring vegetable broth to a rapid boil in a medium saucepan. Listen for the steady rolling boil that signals maximum heat."
- "2 Immediately lower heat to the lowest setting before adding couscous. Stir in couscous, lime juice, zest, and salt quickly but evenly to avoid lumps. The liquid will absorb rapidly; you'll see steam thin and surface calm within moments."
- "3 Cover saucepan tightly and remove from heat. Let stand exactly 4 minutes. Avoid peeking – steam trapped inside finishes cooking grains perfectly, resulting in light, fluffy texture. Over-steaming makes couscous gummy."
- "4 After resting, add olive oil, dill, and toasted pine nuts straightaway. Fluff gently with a fork, incorporating oil and herbs without smashing the grains. Should look fluffy, grains separate, no mush."
- "5 Taste for balance. If too tart, a small pinch of sugar evens acidity without masking lime brightness. Adjust salt as needed."
- "6 Serve warm or room temperature. Great alongside grilled fish or spicy meats."

ingredientsNote: "Substitute vegetable broth with chicken broth if preferred for a meatier taste. Lime can be replaced by lemon if a subtler citrus is wanted. Parsley can be interchanged with dill or cilantro based on freshness and availability; each herb provides distinct flavor profiles. Pine nuts are optional but highly recommended for texture; almonds or walnuts work if pine nuts unavailable but expect different taste and crunch. Olive oil quality matters; extra virgin for mouthfeel and aroma. Salt is crucial for flavor balance, kosher or sea salt preferred over table salt to regulate salinity evenly. If desired, a pinch of smoked paprika stirred in post-fluffing introduces a subtle smoky depth that pairs well with grilled proteins."
instructionsNote: "Watch broth’s surface before adding couscous; it needs to be fully boiling—not just steaming. Dropping couscous into less-than-boiling liquid results in gummy, sticky grains. Stir immediately and evenly. Once covered, do not lift lid or stir during resting; trapped steam finishes cooking without overhydrating. Timing can vary by heat source; four minutes is guideline but judging fluffiness after fluffing matters most. Use a fork, never a spoon, for fluffing—fork tines separate grains without crushing. Adding oil after resting preserves light texture and adds shine. Toast pine nuts in dry skillet over medium heat for 2–3 minutes until golden and fragrant. Watch closely to prevent burning; nuts go from fragrant to bitter fast. Season at the end with salt and a pinch of sugar if acidity too sharp to fine-tune the final taste exactly."
