ingredients:
- "1.5 pounds ground pork (sub ground turkey or lean ground beef)"
- "1 small yellow onion chopped fine"
- "3 cloves garlic minced"
- "2 cups beef broth (or chicken broth in a pinch)"
- "1 can (14 oz) fire-roasted diced tomatoes, undrained"
- "1 tablespoon chili powder"
- "1 tablespoon sherry vinegar (can substitute apple cider vinegar)"
- "1 teaspoon brown sugar"
- "1 tablespoon Worcestershire sauce"
- "0.25 teaspoon cayenne pepper (reduce if sensitive)"
- "0.5 teaspoon ground cinnamon"
- "1 teaspoon ground cumin"
- "0.5 teaspoon salt, adjust later"
- "0.25 teaspoon black pepper freshly ground"
- "0.25 teaspoon ground allspice"
- "0.1 teaspoon ground cloves a pinch"
- "1 dried bay leaf"
instructions:
- "Heat large heavy skillet over medium-high. Add pork, break up with spatula. Brown until edges crisp and color deep, about 5-6 minutes. You want bits sticking to pan—that's flavor foundation. Dump chopped onion and garlic right in, stir, soften for 2-4 minutes, translucent edges signaling it's ready. Drain excess fat if excessive—shouldn't be greasy but some fat locks flavor. Transfer all to large pot or Dutch oven."
- "Pour in beef broth and fire-roasted tomatoes with juice. Sprinkle in chili powder, sherry vinegar for bright acid, brown sugar to balance, Worcestershire for umami depth. Add cayenne, cinnamon, cumin, salt, pepper, allspice, cloves—little at a time. Toss in bay leaf. Stir until uniform. Smell should shift, cinnamon and spices warming the air. Simmer uncovered over low-low heat. Barely a bubble. Slow thickening, flavor melding takes 65-70 minutes. Stir every 15 minutes, watch for sticking or thick spots."
- "Bay leaf removal just before serving. Taste test critical: add salt or vinegar here if flavor dull. Should be tangy, slightly sweet, spicy but balanced. If sauce too thin, turn heat up slightly in last 5 minutes to reduce faster. Too thick? Splash broth or water. Can hold on warm or reheat gently, stir to avoid drying at edges."
- "Serve over hot cooked spaghetti noodles traditionally but try over jasmine rice or baked potatoes for twist. Top hearty shredded sharp cheddar, a light handful of kidney beans for texture and bite, and minced white onion for fresh crunch. Do not drown in toppings; balance is key. Sprinkle cheese so it melts on contact. Beans optional but classic for that still-soft contrast."
- "If pork not available, lean beef or turkey step in. Ground bison or lamb bring new depth too but alter spice balance slightly. Worried about acidity? Cut vinegar by half, add splash more sugar. Need smoky? Stir in chipotle powder or smoked paprika substitute cayenne. Garlic replacing 1 clove with garlic powder possible if fresh not handy."
- "The key: don’t rush low simmer, patient stirring, watch that bay leaf—overstepping leads to bitterness. Browning meat good but avoid burning—bitter tough spots ruin base. Notice onions going glassy, not brown, that's sweetening beginning. Garlic softens fast, do not let it burn. Remember, allspice and cloves potent; small amounts. Add spices in layers. Final flavor adjusts after hour of simmering, trust your palate."
